Here's an overview of our project.

Former president Rodrigo Duterte's infamous War on Drugs is a source of great controversy, and as a result, the strong subject of internet traffic. However, this traffic is not evenly distributed: Google Trends data shows that Philippine search queries involving the keywords "War on Drugs" peaked around the middle of 2017—Kian delos Santos's death.


Did this significant peak of online traffic towards the war on drugs also cause a rise in fake news tweets?

Research question

Can the peak of war on drugs related mis/disinformation be associated with a specific event?

Null hypothesis

There is no specific event which caused a peak in war on drugs related mis/disinformation.

Alternative hypothesis

Kian delos Santos’s death caused a peak in war on drugs related mis/disinformation.

Action plan

Analyze the frequency of posting time of war on drugs related mis/disinformation

Data Preprocessing

How did we ensure that there were no missing values?

All the relevant information taken from a War on Drugs mis/disinformation tweet is acquired from data collection: this includes the Tweet content (allowing us to review the Tweet for data collection), account handle, Tweet URL (as proof the Tweet existed at some point in data collection), and date posted.

The dataset was also quickly checked after data collection through Python to guarantee that none of the tweets have missing values for any of the necessary information. This can be seen in the attached Python Notebook below.

How did we handle outliers?

In a time-series analysis, outliers are generally referred to as extremely high values. As our research question involves checking peaks in Tweet frequency, it is important to keep high frequencies in our data set.

Outliers can also exist by being outside of the time period under study (in our case, from 2016 to 2022), which can be checked through Python by finding the minimum and maximum values of the date posted. No outliers were found through this step.

How did we ensure format consistency?

Tweets are reviewed by hand by us researchers. Aside from human review, Python was used to ensure string consistency (for example, dates can be guaranteed to be formatted as <number><number><slash><number><number>), as well as low level checking for dates and months (for example, months should not go beyond '12'). Both Python and human review ensure formatting is what it needs to be.

Did we normalize/standardize/scale our data?

As this study tackles only one variable (time of Tweet), there is no need to normalize or scale.

How did we categorically encode our data?

While this study tackles numerical data in dates, some relevant information is still categorial. For anonymity, account handles have been assigned a unique ID number per account through the Pandas library, which will be used for extraneous analysis. Each individual Tweet is also assigned a unique ID during collection for anonymous reference during analysis.

Time Series Analysis

How did we perform interpolation on our data?

As of the moment, interpolation does not seem useful to the study. However, a quick linear interpolation of the amount of tweets per month was generated through python, although not particularly informative.

How did we perform binning on our data?

With regards to binning, we have decided to bin our tweets in three ways. The first is to bin tweets as “Disinformation tweets around the time of Kian delos Santos’s death” and “Disinformation tweets during all other time periods.”

The second way is to bin them as “Disinformation tweets before the time of Kian delos Santos’s death,” “Disinformation tweets around the time of Kian delos Santos’s death,” and “Disinformation tweets after the time of Kian delos Santos’s death.”

The third way is to bin them according to K-means clustering, which means we will determine what type of binning gives us the tightest groups.


Other Characteristics

What is the size/shape of the dataset?

After preprocessing, but before feature generation, the dataset has 60 Tweets (rows) and 25 features (columns). Features can be increased as needed for further analysis.

How many unique accounts are in the dataset?

The 60 tweets collected come from 51 unique accounts.

What is the range of the dates posted?

Tweets collected range from February 2016 to June 2022.

How does the data compare to historical events?

The point of the study is to check if a specific event caused the peak of War on Drugs related mis/disinformation, so this question will be tackled in greater depth later in the research process.

However, a initial shortlist of events has been created (and added to if needed) to check for these correlations later on. These include the 2016 elections, the death of Kian delos Santos, Leila de Lima's detention, "ninja cops", and the 2022 elections.


Visualization

What plots did we use?

Various histograms have been created from the dataset with various indices, such as by year or by month. Additionally, histograms have been created for the temporary bins discussed in the bins section (except clustering).

A line graph is useful for time-series analysis as trends between timestamps are easier represented. A simple line graph was created through python (where points are connected through piecewise linear lines), and as needed, line graphs of greater complexity (for example, through interpolation), can also be created. However, such a graph does not seem particularly useful for the study as of now, thus only a linear line graph was created.

Can we use heat maps/swarm plots/violin plots?

The above graphs cannot be created from our dataset as only one variable is relevant to the study, which is time posted.


Features

What statistical tests do we plan on using?

The statistical tests we will likely use are the Mann-Whitney U Test and the Kruskal-Wallis Test.

For the Mann-Whitney U Test, we’ll be defining our two groups as “Disinformation tweets around the time of Kian delos Santos’s death” and “Disinformation tweets during all other time periods.” This test will tell us if there is a significant difference in disinformation tweet counts between the time period of Kian’s death, and all other time periods.

For the Kruskal-Wallis Test, we will define three groups, “Disinformation tweets before the time of Kian delos Santos’s death,” “Disinformation tweets around the time of Kian delos Santos’s death,” and “Disinformation tweets after the time of Kian delos Santos’s death.” This test will tell us if there is a significant difference in disinformation tweet counts among the three time periods, and then also be able to further analyze with other tests to determine which specific pairs of groups differ significantly.

We also plan on using the K-means clustering machine learning algorithm to find the best way to cluster/group our tweets. We will vary the number of clusters and determine which number will yield the tightest clusters. Through this, we will be able to determine if there indeed was a cluster around the time of Kian delos Santos’s death and also be able to compare the sizes of each cluster.

What features did we add to the dataset?

Aside from replacing account handles with ID numbers, new features can be generated through splits of the date posted: by time, month, date, or year. More features can also be added depending on the binning process/cluster generation.
