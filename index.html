Overview of the Project

Former president Rodrigo Duterte's infamous War on Drugs is a source of great controversy, and as a result, the strong subject of internet traffic. However, this traffic is not evenly distributed: Google Trends data shows that Philippine search queries involving the keywords "war on drugs" peaked around the middle of 2017—around the time of Kian delos Santos's death.

  • Research Question

    Can the peak of war-on-drugs-related mis/disinformation be associated with a specific event?

  • Hypotheses

    H0 | No specific event caused a peak in war-on-drugs-related mis/disinformation.

    H1 | Kian delos Santos’s death caused the peak in war-on-drugs-related mis/disinformation.

  • Action Plan

    Analyze the frequency of posting time of war-on-drugs-related mis/disinformation

Collecting the Data

  • 110,000+ Scraped
  • 30,000+ Sampled
  • 155 Dataset

Snscrape was used to collect a batch of tweets using war-on-drugs-related keywords, such as the various ways to spell Duterte (like "DU30" or "PRRD" ), different terms for drugs ("shabu, droga, meth" ), and war-on-drugs-specific keywords such as "tokhang". As this batch is far too large (approximately 110,000 tweets large), a random sample of the batch was scoured by-hand for war-on-drugs-related mis/disinformation. At the end of the process, 155 mis/disinformation tweets were acquired.

Exploring the Data

Data Preprocessing

How did we ensure that there were no missing values?

All the relevant information taken from a war on drugs mis/disinformation tweet is acquired from data collection. This includes the tweet content (allowing us to review the tweet for data collection), account handle, tweet URL (as proof the tweet existed at some point in data collection), and date posted. The dataset was also quickly checked after data collection through Python to guarantee that none of the tweets have missing values for any of the necessary information. This can be seen in the attached Python Notebook below.

How did we handle outliers?

In a time-series analysis, outliers are generally referred to as extremely high values. As our research question involves checking peaks in tweet frequency, it is important to keep high frequencies in our data set. Outliers can also exist by being outside of the time period under study (in our case, from 2016 to 2022), which can be checked through Python by finding the minimum and maximum values of the date posted. No outliers were found through this step.

How did ensure format consistency?

Tweets are reviewed by hand by us researchers. Aside from human review, Python was used to ensure string consistency. For example, dates were verified to be formatted as:
<number><number><slash><number><number> Additionally, low level checking was also performed for dates and months (e.g., months should not go beyond '12'). Both Python and human review ensure formatting is what it needs to be.

Did we normalize/standardize/scale our data?

As this study tackles only one variable (time of tweet), there is no need to normalize or scale.

How did we categorically encode our data?

While this study tackles numerical data in dates, some relevant information is still categorial. For anonymity, account handles were assigned a unique ID number per account through the Pandas library, which will then be used for extraneous analysis. Each individual tweet is also assigned a unique ID during collection for anonymous reference during analysis.

How did we perform interpolation on our data?

When our dataset was much smaller, it initially seemed like we would need to perform interpolation. There are a few interpolation methods of note and their graphs can be seen below. Note that some methods (quadratic and cubic interpolation) add negative values in order to generate a proper smooth curve for the positive peaks.

Based on the graph above, piecewise polynomial interpolation seems the most promising. This is because it does not use negative values, is not as simple and unrealistic as linear, and does not copy other data values despite missing endpoints. This can be seen better in the isolated graph below.

How did we perform binning on our data?

We binned our data in terms of what we consider to be "part of the event." The tweets were binned as “Disinformation tweets before the time of Kian delos Santos’s death,” “Disinformation tweets around the time of Kian delos Santos’s death,” and “Disinformation tweets after the time of Kian delos Santos’s death.”

Modeling the Data

Statistical Modelling

As we are comparing counts, the statistical test of choice is the chi-square test for homogeneity. The chi-square test can compare the count of different populations and check if they are homogeneous by comparing them to some expected frequency. In order for our data to fit the statistical test, the mis/disinformation tweets are binned into three: before the event (January 2016 to July 2017), during the event (August 2017 to October 2017), and after the event (November 2017 to December 2022), with the event being Kian delos Santos’s death. As each bin has varying durations (the before, during, and after bins last 19, 3, and 62 months respectively), the expected frequency to be used in the test should be scaled accordingly. Thus, the expected frequency is scaled as the total number of tweets (155) times the duration of the bin divided by the total length of the data (84 months total).

Null Hypothesis There are no differences between the classes of before, during, and after the event.
Alternate Hypothesis There are differences between the classes of before, during, and after the event.

If the highest peak of mis/disinformation tweets is found during the event, then it is expected that the frequency of tweets during the event is significantly different compared to before and after the event. As the chi-square test only gives information regarding the differences between classes and does not specify which class is higher or lower, the same process as above can be applied for post-hoc testing for each pair in the 3 bins to glean further information. The statistical tests are computed through SciPy. Note that SciPy has checks for various assumptions for the data (for example, having both the test frequencies and the expected frequencies be above a certain value), thus no assumptions have been broken for the chi-square test.

Computational Modelling

After calculating the number of mis/disinformation tweets per month, this can be graphed as a line graph spanning from January 2016 to December 2022, as seen in the data exploration portion above. Two methods will then be performed on this data to gain more insight about the nature of the data: peak detection and change-point detection. Peak detection is done using SciPy’s built-in peak detection function. This simply detects where in the graph there are significant peaks which can then be associated with an event. Change-point detection is done through the Pruned Exact Linear Time (PELT) algorithm. Change-points segment the time-series into areas where each segment is a new model; that is, between segments, the graph has changed significantly.

The above algorithms detected 8 peaks and 6 change-points, all of which can be associated with some event below. These real-life events aligning with peaks in the graph (as well as also aligning with some segments of the Google Trends graph) help provide evidence that the model for mis/disinformation tweets is grounded in reality.

August 2017 Kian Delos Santos, a 17-year-old highschool student was reportedly killed by two policemen. The police claim he had a gun and shot at them while witnesses say he was unarmed.

Results & Discussion

The p-value for the chi-square test between all three bins is approximately 0.000008. Thus, there is sufficient evidence to conclude that there was a difference in the frequencies of each bin. However, a pairwise chi-square test between the before and during bins has a p-value approximately equal to 0.135, showing that there is insufficient evidence to reject the hypothesis that there is no difference in the classes of tweets before the event and during the event. In this sense, the frequency of the before bin is not significantly different from the event bin, therefore it cannot be concluded that Kian delos Santos’s death caused the highest peak in mis/disinformation tweets. This is supported by the machine learning graph, which shows while there exists a peak for Kian delos Santos’s death, the peak for the tweets before the event (particularly due to the elections) is higher.

Conclusion

All in all, the testing shows that the highest peak in War on Drugs mis/disinformation is not from Kian delos Santos’s death, and no new evidence to support the claim that Google Trends data and Twitter mis/disinformation tweets are related has been created.

Possible Implications

There are two main possible implications from the conclusion of this study, with regards to the correlation between Google Trends and the frequency of mis/disinformation tweets. Note that further research would need to be done to delve into either of these two working ideas. One possibility is that the relationship between Google Trends and the spread of mis/disinformation is not simply linear. It could be argued that people will spread more mis/disinformation in circumstances where there is more to gain. There might not be much to gain out of spreading mis/disinformation when someone is unjustly killed due to the war on drugs. However, there is a lot more to gain when doing the same during an election season, which could very well decide the presidency. Another possibility has more to do with how Google Trends defines topics. Recall that the Google Trends graph mentioned earlier lists “war on drugs.” There may be a spike in interest due to Kian delos Santos’ death as this issue is directly related to said war on drugs. However, the discussion including war on drugs during the election season might not be considered as interest in “war on drugs” specifically, as people are likely also discussing Duterte's other planned projects and presidency as a whole. Google Trends may classify this as interest in “President Duterte,” rather than just “war on drugs.”
